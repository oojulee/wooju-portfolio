RePlate

RePlate is an app designed to connect food donors with recipients in need, reducing food waste and addressing food insecurity by creating a simple, community-driven platform for sharing surplus food.

What?

As a college student, it is hard to manage cooking, eating, and food waste.

Why??

  • Busy schedule
  • Financial Reasons
  • Knowledge of cooking

How might we

How might we help college students cook more efficiently, minimize food waste, and reduce food insecurity?

User Persona

Alex

Age: 28  |  Location: Davis, CA
Occupation: Graduate Student in Cognitive Science

Alex is a busy grad student who often skips meals due to financial stress. He is aware of food waste issues on campus and wants a convenient way to access donated meals between classes.

Frustrations

  • Doesn't know where to find free or low-cost food near campus.
  • Feels stigma around using food assistance programs.
  • Existing apps are too complicated or location-inaccurate.

Goals

  • Quickly find nearby food donations with minimal effort.
  • Receive notifications when food is available close to him.
  • Feel part of a supportive community rather than charity.

User Survey

Key Findings

  • 78% of respondents said they would use a food-sharing app if it were available.
  • 65% reported throwing away food at least once a week.
  • 52% said they would donate food if the process were simple enough.
  • Most users preferred a map-based interface to find nearby donations.

Survey bar charts

Survey participants highlighted ease-of-use and real-time notifications as the most important features for a food-sharing app. Privacy and anonymity for recipients was also a top concern.

Interview

We conducted interviews with 8 participants across Davis and Sacramento who had either donated or received food through community programs. Key insights surfaced around trust, convenience, and the social dynamics of food sharing.

Key Quotes

  • "I want to know the food is safe before I pick it up."
  • "I'd donate more often if someone could pick it up from me."
  • "I feel awkward asking for free food — it should feel more normal."

Ideation & Low-Fidelity

Based on our user research insights, we created solutions for possible features. We mapped out key user flows — donating, browsing, requesting, and tracking — and sketched initial wireframe screens to validate the core navigation structure before moving to digital tools.

  • Map-first home screen showing nearby donations
  • Simple two-step donate flow with photo upload
  • Anonymous request mode for recipients
  • In-app chat and pickup scheduling
Low-fidelity wireframes

Wire Fidelity & Usability Testing

Onboarding

Users can set up a profile quickly by selecting their role — donor, recipient, or volunteer — and granting location access. Testers found the onboarding clear and completed it in under 90 seconds.

Usability Feedback:

  • Users wanted a "skip for now" option on the notification prompt.
  • Profile photo felt optional — make it explicit.
Onboarding wireframe

Browse & Request

The browse screen shows a map with pins for available food items nearby. Tapping a pin reveals a card with photo, distance, and estimated freshness. Users can request directly from the card.

Usability Feedback:

  • Map pins needed clearer category icons (cooked vs. packaged).
  • Users wanted a list view alternative to the map.
Browse wireframe

Donate Food

Donors post a listing in three steps: snap a photo, describe the item and quantity, then set a pickup window. The simplified flow tested well — average completion time was 45 seconds.

Usability Feedback:

  • Users wanted to duplicate a previous listing for recurring donations.
  • Expiry time field caused confusion — changed to a dropdown with presets.
Donate wireframe

Notifications & Messages

Push notifications alert recipients when food they may like becomes available nearby. In-app messaging lets donors and recipients coordinate pickup details without sharing personal contact info.

Usability Feedback:

  • Users wanted granular notification controls (radius, food type).
  • Message thread needed a "food claimed" status banner.
Notifications wireframe

Profile

User profiles display donation history, ratings, and a trust badge system. Recipients can view a donor's track record before accepting food, increasing platform-wide confidence.

Usability Feedback:

  • Rating system needed clearer explanation of the criteria.
  • Users wanted to see total food weight donated as a personal impact stat.
Profile wireframe

High-Fidelity

After incorporating usability testing feedback, we produced a high-fidelity prototype in Figma. The final design uses a warm, approachable color palette with green as the primary accent, emphasizing freshness and sustainability.

Find a Food Donor

The home screen presents a live map of nearby food listings. Users can filter by food type, distance, and dietary needs. Each listing shows a photo, distance, and time until expiry.

Find a Food Donor screen

Receive a Donation

Tapping a food listing opens the detail view: photo gallery, ingredient list, quantity, pickup instructions, and donor rating. One tap sends a request; the donor receives a notification instantly.

Receive a Donation screen

RePlate — Donate

Donors post surplus food in under a minute using the guided listing flow. Smart suggestions help fill in common food names based on photo recognition, reducing friction for first-time donors.

Donate screen

Create a Profile

User profiles show impact stats (total donations, meals provided), a trust badge, and reviews. Verified badges are awarded after identity confirmation to build community trust.

Profile screen

Challenges

Throughout the RePlate project, several design challenges required careful consideration and iteration.

Balancing Simplicity and Features

Early prototypes were overloaded with filters and options. User testing revealed that most participants wanted a fast, two-tap experience. We reduced the home screen to a single map view with a floating action button for donating, cutting the average task time by 40%.

Building Trust Between Strangers

Food safety concerns were the biggest barrier to adoption. We introduced a verified badge system, food-safety guidelines in the listing flow, and a report function, which together raised perceived trustworthiness scores in testing by 35%.

Addressing Stigma

Many recipients felt uncomfortable with traditional food-assistance aesthetics. We adopted warm, community-first language ("share" and "give" instead of "donate" and "receive"), and positioned RePlate as a sustainability app rather than a charity platform.

Next Steps

  • Expand the volunteer driver network for pickup-only listings.
  • Integrate with local food banks for larger-scale surplus distribution.
  • Add an impact dashboard showing community-wide food rescued stats.
  • Conduct further accessibility testing with screen-reader users.
